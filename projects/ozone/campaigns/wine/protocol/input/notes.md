# SECTION 1 — SYSTEM ENTRY POINT

## What This Ozone Layer Is — and How to Use It Correctly

**Reader:** A winery operator who already has a functioning grape dehydration process but still experiences unpredictable microbial spoilage, mold outbreaks, or instability during drying.

**Goal of this section:**
Get you to **integrate ozone into your existing process correctly by understanding exactly where it fits, what it controls, and what parts of your current system must remain unchanged.**

## This product is not a dehydration system

You already have a dehydration process.

That means you already have:

* airflow
* humidity management
* temperature control
* drying timelines
* grape handling procedures

This protocol does not replace those systems.

It adds a controlled ozone layer on top of them.

Your objective is not to redesign drying.

Your objective is to:

* reduce microbial instability during dehydration
* suppress mold pressure during long drying cycles
* stabilize the room biologically without changing your core process

## The ozone layer has only two functions

This protocol uses ozone in two distinct ways:

### 1. High-intensity sanitation (short duration)

A one-time operation performed immediately after grape loading.

Purpose:

* rapidly reduce microbial load in the room and on exposed surfaces
* establish a low-contamination starting condition

This is a reset phase.

### 2. Low-level maintenance ozone (continuous)

A minimal ozone output maintained throughout the drying cycle.

Purpose:

* suppress microbial regrowth during dehydration
* maintain environmental stability over time

This is not active sanitation.

It is a maintenance layer.

## What you are NOT trying to do

Do not use ozone to:

* accelerate dehydration
* compensate for poor airflow
* fix overloaded grape placement
* correct unstable humidity conditions
* rescue already contaminated batches

This protocol only works when:

* your existing dehydration process already functions mechanically
* ozone is used as a microbial control layer, not a correction tool

## The 4-step operating sequence

You will apply ozone in the same order every cycle:

### Step 1 — Prepare the room

Clean and sanitize the drying environment so ozone can work effectively.

### Step 2 — Load the grapes

Place grapes so airflow and ozone exposure remain uniform across the room.

### Step 3 — Apply a strong ozone sanitation pulse

Run a short-duration high-intensity ozone cycle immediately after loading.

This establishes the low-microbial baseline.

### Step 4 — Maintain minimal ozone during dehydration

Run continuous low-output ozone during the drying cycle to suppress microbial growth over time.

This maintains environmental stability.

## Your rule for using this protocol

Once the cycle begins:

* do not improvise
* do not adjust ozone randomly
* do not react to every small variation
* do not change process structure mid-cycle

Consistency is more important than intensity.

## Before continuing to Step 1

Confirm the following:

* Your drying process already functions operationally
* You are adding ozone as a microbial stability layer
* You are willing to run the same sequence every batch
* You are not expecting ozone to compensate for mechanical process failures

If all four are true, continue to Section 2.

# STEP 1 — PREPARE (ROOM RESET)

## Action: Make the room ozone-ready

### 1. Remove organic load

* Remove all grapes, stems, residues, dust
* Clear all collection points
* Empty waste outside facility

**PASS:** No organic material present anywhere

---

### 2. Clean all surfaces

Targets:

* racks, trays
* floors
* walls (up to 2 m)
* fans, vents, accessible ducts

Method:

* detergent wash (food-grade alkaline)
* full rinse (no residue)
* complete drying before next step

**PASS:** No visible residue or moisture film

---

### 3. Open airflow paths

* Remove obstructions near vents/fans (≥50 cm clearance)
* Ensure full air circulation pathways are open

**PASS:** No blocked airflow zones

---

### 4. Seal room

* Close all external access points
* Disable external air exchange
* Set internal recirculation (if available)

**PASS:** Closed, isolated environment

---

# STEP 2 — CARICA (GRAPE LOADING)

## Action: Load grapes for uniform exposure

* Maintain spacing between clusters (no contact)
* Use uniform layer depth across racks
* Avoid dense accumulation zones
* Align loading with airflow direction
* Avoid corner and wall congestion

**PASS CONDITION:**

* Uniform density
* Full airflow accessibility
* No contact clusters
* No blocked zones

---

# STEP 3 — SHOCK (HIGH-INTENSITY OZONE RESET)

## Action: One-time microbial reset after loading

### Operating conditions:

* Ozone: **20–40 ppm**
* Duration: **30–60 minutes**
* Air circulation: ON
* Room: fully sealed

### Procedure:

1. Activate internal airflow
2. Start ozone at full sanitation mode
3. Run uninterrupted cycle
4. Do not open or adjust during operation

### Post-cycle:

* Ventilate until ozone is fully cleared before proceeding

**PASS CONDITION:**

* Low microbial baseline established across air and surfaces

# SECTION 5 — CONTINUOUS LOW-LEVEL OZONE CONTROL

## Step 4: Maintain Minimal Ozone During Drying

**Reader:** A winery operator producing dried-grape wines who has already completed room preparation, correct grape placement, and a high-intensity ozone reset, and now needs to maintain microbial stability throughout the long drying cycle.

**Goal of this section:**
Get you to **run a continuous, low-level ozone output during the entire dehydration process to suppress microbial regrowth without disrupting drying conditions.**

---

## Do this immediately after the high-intensity reset (Step 3)

Once the ozone reset is complete and the room has stabilized, you transition into the maintenance phase.

This is the longest and most important control layer in the system.

---

## Step 1: Activate low-level continuous ozone output

Set ozone to a **minimal, steady output level** designed for maintenance only.

Your rule:

* output must be constant, not fluctuating
* no intermittent bursts or manual cycling
* no reactive changes during the batch

This is a stability layer, not a sanitation event.

---

## Step 2: Run ozone continuously for the full drying cycle

Maintenance ozone must remain active:

* from immediately after Step 3
* until the end of the drying process

Do not turn it off during the cycle.

Interruptions create microbial recovery windows that lead to instability.

---

## Step 3: Maintain airflow compatibility

During continuous ozone operation:

* airflow must remain stable and consistent
* circulation must reach all zones equally
* no new blockages or system changes should be introduced

Ozone effectiveness depends on uniform distribution, not concentration spikes.

---

## Step 4: Do not adjust ozone in response to short-term changes

Do not modify output based on:

* temporary humidity fluctuations
* perceived drying speed changes
* localized visual differences in grape condition

Only adjust if there is a confirmed system failure, not normal variation.

---

## Step 5: Keep environmental systems stable alongside ozone

Maintenance ozone works only when:

* temperature remains within your defined operating range
* humidity stays stable within target limits
* airflow structure remains unchanged

Do not try to “correct” environmental shifts by changing ozone behavior.

---

## Output you must maintain throughout this step

During the entire drying cycle:

* microbial pressure remains continuously suppressed
* no unchecked regrowth occurs in stagnant or dense zones
* drying progresses under stable biological conditions

---

## What you do NOT do in this step

* Do not turn ozone on and off during the cycle
* Do not increase intensity mid-process
* Do not use ozone as a corrective tool for spoilage already forming
* Do not change airflow or environmental settings reactively
* Do not treat this as an active sanitation phase

---

## Why this step matters (in one line)

Without continuous low-level ozone, microbial populations recover between interventions and reintroduce unpredictable spoilage during long drying cycles.
